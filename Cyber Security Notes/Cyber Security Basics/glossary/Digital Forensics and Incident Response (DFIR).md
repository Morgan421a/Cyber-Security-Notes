**Digital Forensics:**

Analyses evidence of an attack and its perpetrators as well as other areas including:

- Intellectual Property Theft
- Cyber Espionage
- Possession of Unauthorised Content

Digital Forensics focuses on different areas such as:

- File system: Analysing a digital [[forensics image]] of a system's storage reveals much information, such as installed programs, created files, partially overwritten files and deleted files.
- System Memory: If an attacker runs a malicious program without saving it to the disk, taking a forensic image  of the system memory is the best way to analyse its content and learn about the attack.
- System logs: Even if an attacker tries to clear their traces the log files will still hold some traces of their activities.
- Network Logs: Logs of the network packets that traversed a network can make clearer if an attack is occurring and what it entails.

**Incident Response:**

Specifies the method that should be followed to handle an incident.

An incident typically refers to a data breach or a cyber attack, but in some cases it can be something less critical, such as a misconfiguration, an intrusion attempt or a policy violation.

There are four major phases of the incident response process:

1. Preparation: Requires a trained team, ready to deal with incidents.
2. Detection and Analysis: The team has the resources needed to detect any incident; they also analyse any detected incident further to learn of its severity.
3. Containment, Eradication and Recovery: Once detected an incident should be isolated to stop it from affecting other systems. then eliminate it and recover the affected systems. 
4. Post-Incident Activity: Produce a report post-recovery detailing what was learned from the incident and share it to prevent similar incidents in the future.
